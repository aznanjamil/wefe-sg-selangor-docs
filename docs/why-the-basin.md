# Why The Basin

The **Why The Basin** page tells the story of why the Sungai Selangor basin matters — to households, to industry, to ecosystems, and to the climate-resilience of the Klang Valley.

> ![](/img/site/why-basin-overview.png)

## The Basin in Numbers

The Sungai Selangor Basin covers **1,955 km²** and is a critical water source for the Klang Valley. Key figures include:

- **2,950 MLD** — Current water supply from the Sungai Selangor Water Supply Scheme
- **~60%** — Share of Kuala Lumpur and Selangor's total water needs supplied by the basin
- **~67%** — Share of Klang Valley's surface water demand met by the basin
- **0%** — Spare upstream freshwater available for new users (the basin is fully allocated)
- **2,934 MLD** — River water flowing unused to the sea due to insufficient capture infrastructure

## Who Depends on the Basin

The basin supports a closely connected **Water–Energy–Food–Environment (WEFE)** system:

- **Households** — The basin supplies drinking water to millions in the Klang Valley. Per capita consumption averaged **227 litres per day** (2016–2019), well above the WHO recommendation of **165 litres per day**.
- **Industry & Energy** — Water treatment and pumping operations (e.g., OPAK system with 33 pumps at Bestari Jaya). Pumping demand is highest during dry years, when the electrical grid is also under pressure from increased cooling demand.
- **Agriculture & Food** — Aquaculture is the dominant agricultural water user (88% of non-WTP surface water abstraction), with 54 freshwater aquaculture abstractors in Hulu Selangor alone. Paddy farming and livestock (~7.4 million chickens across Hulu Selangor, Kuala Selangor, and Gombak) also depend on basin water.
- **Ecosystems** — Upper catchment forests (Hutan Simpan Hulu Selangor, Hutan Simpan Semangkok), Raja Musa peat swamp (~70,000 ha), coastal mangroves, and the Kampung Kuantan firefly sanctuary (1,000 ha, gazetted 2009).

## Pressures and Trends

The basin faces growing pressures that make integrated management essential:

### Water Stress
- **Fully allocated** — All surface water upstream of Bestari Jaya is already assigned; no spare water for new users without reducing existing allocations
- **Non-Revenue Water (NRW)** — Stood at **29.6% in 2019** (SPAN target: 25% by 2023; Air Selangor target: 20% by 2037). A significant portion is attributable to **6,196 km of aging asbestos-cement pipes** in the distribution network.
- **High per capita consumption** — Domestic water is free up to 20 m³ per month, reducing the financial incentive for households to conserve.

### Climate Variability
- **Below-average rainfall** — 17% below long-term average in 2019; dam levels fell and emergency pumping was activated
- **Droughts and floods** — Increasingly variable rainfall patterns projected under climate change
- **Pollution incidents** — Illegal chemical dumping (July 2019, 9.5-hour shutdown, 879 MLD loss), oil spills from sand-mining pontoons, and sediment from uncontrolled earthworks causing repeated treatment plant shutdowns

### Environmental Degradation
- **Forest disturbance** — Development pressures in upper catchments (e.g., Rawang Bypass Highway originally planned to affect 65 ha of Kanching Protected Forest, later reduced to 24 ha through viaduct redesign)
- **Peat swamp drainage** — Agricultural drainage lowers groundwater tables and reduces the Raja Musa peat swamp's buffering capacity
- **Mangrove and firefly decline** — FRIM monitoring showed a **30% decline in firefly populations from 2007 to 2019**, driven mainly by land clearing between 2007 and 2009. Gradual recovery has been observed since habitat rehabilitation began in 2011.

## Why Integrated WEFE Thinking Matters

Water, energy, food, and the environment in the basin are deeply interconnected:

- **Water → Energy** — Using more water requires more energy for pumping and treatment. The OPAK system operates 33 pumps during droughts, precisely when grid pressure is highest.
- **Energy → Water** — The HORAS 600 pond was built partly to reduce emergency pumping operations, improving efficiency across both sectors.
- **Food → Environment** — Expanding agriculture can reduce forest cover and weaken natural water filtration. Chicken farms discharging untreated effluent are a non-point source of river pollution.
- **Environment → Water** — Upper catchment forests feed dams with filtered, low-turbidity water. When forest cover is disturbed, turbidity rises and treatment costs increase.
- **Environment → Food** — Saltwater intrusion during low-flow periods threatens freshwater aquaculture and coastal fisheries. Mangroves buffer against this intrusion.

> **Key insight:** The basin does not have a water availability problem alone — it has a **capture and storage problem**. The 2,934 MLD flowing unused to the sea is approximately **99% of the current scheme's supply capacity**. Improving efficiency and building strategic storage are both essential.
